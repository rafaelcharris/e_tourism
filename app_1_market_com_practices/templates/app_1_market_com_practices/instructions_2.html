You were assigned to the role of seller. Remember that you will keep this role during the 5 trading rounds. You will be able to sell only one package per trading round. The computer will randomly assign you one of the 5 available packages for you to sell in the market. Keep in mind that other sellers may sell the same package as you. Additionally, you will be given a "value" of that package. This value represents the cost you would incur if you were to close a transaction.

Keep in mind that the costs are private, that is, only you know this number. Neither the other sellers nor the buyers know this information. In total there are 5 types of tourist packages whose only difference will be the destination: Berlin, Madrid, Vienna, Paris and Rome. You will only receive one of these and its respective cost.

You will have to fix a price for the package that was assigned to you. This price must be higher than the cost of the package. Your earnings will be calculated as the difference between the price you set and the cost of the package. If you fail to complete a transaction, you will not incur any costs, but you will not make any profit either. Think of it this way, let's suppose you have a chance to get the package in question, and if you manage. to sell it, you'll earn the difference. The cost is what you would have to pay to get the package. If the cost is 50 ECU and you manage to sell the package for 70 ECU, then your profit would be 20 ECU (plus the initial endowment of {{ Constants.endowment }} ECU). If you do not sell the package, your profit would be zero (plus the initial endowment of {{ Constants.endowment }} ECU), i.e. you would not incur in any costs and would not receive any income.

Let's see another example:

Suppose the cost of your package is 30 ECU and you manage to sell the package at a price of 50 ECU. You will then earn 50 ECU - 30 ECU = 20 ECU (plus the initial endowment of {{ Constants.endowment }} ECU). If you fail to close a transaction, you will not incur in any no cost nor profit, i.e. your profit will be 0 ECU (plus the initial endowment of {{ Constants.endowment }} ECU)

It is important to note that two buyers can select the same offer, but only one of them will complete the transaction. The one who makes the decision to buy the package the fastest will be the one who completes the transaction with you.

You were assigned to the role of buyer. Remember that you will keep this role during the 5 trading rounds. You will be able to buy only one package per trading round. The computer will give you a "value" for each of the packages you will find for sale in the market. This value represents how much you value that package if you were to close a transaction. In total there are 5 types of tourist packages whose only difference will be the destination: Berlin, Madrid, Vienna, Paris and Rome. You will only receive five values, one for each package.

Please note that the values are private, i.e. only you know this numbers. Neither the other buyers nor the sellers know this information.

You will be shown a list of all package offers in the market and their respective prices. To close a transaction, you must select one and only one of the packages in the market. Your earnings will be calculated as the difference between your valuation and the price at which you purchased the package. If you fail to complete a transaction, you won’t make any profit (plus the initial endowment of {{ Constants.endowment }} ECU).

Think of it this way, suppose you have the ability to resell each of the five packages in question, and if you manage to get one, you will earn the difference. The value is what you would receive when you resell the package. If the value is 70 ECU and you manage to get the package at a price of 50 ECU, then your profit would be 20 ECU (plus the initial endowment of {{ Constants.endowment }} ECU). If you do not succeed in buying the package, your profit would be zero, i.e. you would not pay any price and would not receive any income (except for the initial endowment of {{ Constants.endowment }} ECU).

Let's see another example:

Suppose that the value for the package you decide to buy is 50 ECU and you manage to buy the package at a price of 30 ECU. You will then earn 50 ECU - 30 ECU = 20 ECU (plus the initial endowment of {{ Constants.endowment }} ECU). If you fail to close a transaction, you will not incur in any cost nor profit, i.e. your profit will be 0 ECU (plus the initial endowment of {{ Constants.endowment }} ECU).

It is important to note that two buyers can select the same offer but only one of them will complete the transaction. The one who makes the decision to buy the package the fastest will be the one who completes the transaction.

Additionally, notice that the sellers can choose a commercial practice to help make their packages more attractive in the market. There are three commercial practices and if a seller uses them, they will be visible to the buyer at the time of completing the transaction. However, keep in mind that any of these commercial practices can be used truthfully or untruthfully.

There are three commercial practices available that buyers will be able to use to make their package more attractive to the market.

Commercial Practice Definition Example
Best Price Guarantee This commercial practice allows the seller to make the announcement that, among all packages with the same destination, this one has the lowest price. Suppose the seller offers the package to Rome. There may be other sellers in the market selling the same package to Rome, but if the seller opts for this commercial practice, the seller would be saying that, of all the packages to Rome, hers/his is the cheapest.
Reference Pricing This commercial practice allows the seller to make the announcement that between the first price the seller proposed and the end, the seller offered a discount. Suppose the seller offers a package to Rome. The seller can claim that she/he made a discount between the first price she/he bid of 60 ECU and the final (the one the seller confirmed after deciding whether or not to observe the competition)
Drip pricing This commercial practice allows the seller to display a price for the package she/he is bidding that is less than the total price she/he is asking for it. The difference between the actual price and the price the seller is displaying is equal to the cost for viewing the full list of packages (2 ECU), regardless of whether the seller decided to pay this cost or not. Suppose the seller offers a package to Rome and the price the seller claims for this package is 80 ECU. If the seller decides to use this commercial practice, the seller's offer will appear with a price of 78 ECU*, with a note indicating the total price.

Notice that these commercial practices can be used truthfully (case 1) or untruthfully (case 2). Using the examples in the table above, in the following table you will find each of the commercial practices and the examples for case 1 and case 2.

Commercial Practice Case 1 (truthful) Case 2 (untruthful)
Best Price Guarantee 1) When there is no other package to Rome. 2) When there exists at least another package to Rome and the latter has a larger price. 1) When there exists at least another package to Rome and the latter has a smaller price.
Reference Pricing 1) Use of the real discount. 1) Use of the real discount plus 20 ECU
Drip pricing 1) When the seller shows the price minus the cost to see the first offer of the other sellers. 1) When the seller decides not to see the prices of the competitors but still show the price net of this cost

Remember that it is the seller’s decision whether or not to use any of these commercial practices.
